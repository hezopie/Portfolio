UX Design

Understand/Empathize

We try to get inside the head of users, but we all process information differently. The importance of never making assumptions and getting real people in front of our product is worth the extra time and effort. As an avid internet browser, what may seem obvious to me is not necessarily intuitive for everyone.

  • Research/Define (challenge your assumptions)
  • Interviews
  • Focus Groups
  • Surveys

Usability testing Discover… learn as much about… everything, before anything begins. Once the info is gathered, I organize and document steps and tasks, group by importance, level of effort, the end goal, role, and progress. Next, it’s time to work out the best combination of steps that will achieve the outcome we need that aligns with the mission and goals, and assign the appropriate team member(s) to follow-up and/or brainstorm.

Brainstorming allows my team to evaluate all collected research and requirements, and begin to form conclusions and develop solutions. I try to include as many members of the team as I can in this step. Depending on what we are trying to solve, more often than not, everyone walks away with an assigned task or two, and/or research to followup on. The more engaged team members are, the more vested they are.

I have one steadfast rule for brainstorming… stay stocked up on sticky notes!

I love to see participants sharing well beyond the brainstorming meeting (that’s when I know a meeting is a productive one). Then there are those times that the ideas are just not flowing. Then it’s time to send the team out for a break to get a cup of joe… and try again later.

I used the form below to gather information from a group of testers engaging with our intranet interactive wireframe early on in the project [SHOW AC FORM, MAKE CAROUSEL].

Wireframe with full flow and interactivity, is essential. I like the wireframe to include every page and show every click. Having extensive insight into the experience of users that may interact with the product is an amazing process to walk through.

The wireframe helps my team and the client understand the end product earlier, resulting in less occurrence of major changes later. Being able to provide a visual understanding before the creative phase gets underway is much easier to adapt than concept design. It also serves as a clear guide for a functionality plan, setting milestones, and producing timelines.

QC

Solid research and planning results in smooth flow for the rest of the project. Massive amounts of work can be accomplished at this stage. The development team works on a functionality plan. The design team starts the UI mockup and once approved; development can begin.

This is my favorite stage (progress steps up a notch). And of course, for every hour of work at this stage, there are 2-3 hours of testing and QC (not as fun… but a necessary evil).
