My Process

Step one

Research

Discover… learn as much about… everything, before anything begins. What is required? What can we solve now or later? What requires additional research or testing? What new ideas are there? How can we validate those ideas? What do we know about the user? What don't we know, that we should? What problems do they face?

Once massive amounts of info is gathered, I organize and document steps and tasks, group by importance, level of effort, end goal, role and progress. Next, it’s time to work out the best combination of steps that will achieve the outcome that aligns with the mission and goals. I assign to the appropriate team member(s) to follow-up or brainstorm.

My most successful planning stages are when we involve more people for input and test users early. I've seen projects that reached out for usability input far too late... and parts of development had to be scrapped.

Step two

Brainstorm

Brainstorming allows my team to evaluate all collected research and requirements, and begin to form conclusions and develop hypotheses. I try to include as many members of the team as I can in this step. Depending on what we are trying to solve, more often than not, everyone walks away with an assigned task or two, and/or research to followup on. The more engaged team members are, the more vested they are.

I have one steadfast rule for brainstorming… stay stocked up on sticky notes!

I love to see participants sharing well beyond the brainstorming meeting (that’s when I know a meeting is a productive one). Then there are those times that the ideas are just not flowing. Then it’s time to send the team out for a break to get a cup of joe… and try again later.

Research and evaluate if and how we can integrate with ATS and CRM.

A/B testing – content variations to measure what keeps user on the page (and scrolling) longer.

Coordinate user group to interact and test wireframe.

Setup a HubSpot form for users to sign up for regular emails. Update and create a workflow/drip campaign.

Set up progressive load with 1,000 sample images. Load in bactches triggered by scroll. Measure and optimize speed on various networks.

Research better solution for user to upload documents... perhaps a drag and drop?

Review application process. Shorten to collect required info only. Evaluate and improve user experience overall.

Begin UI mockups from approved wireframes to freshen up outdated design.

Step three

Prototype and identify user experience issues early on.

A prototype of all pages, with full flow and interactivity, is essential. I like the wireframe to include every page and show every click. Having extensive insight into the experience of users that may interact with the product is an amazing process to walk through.

The wireframe helps my team and the client understand the end product earlier, resulting in less occurrence of major changes later. Being able to provide a visual understanding before the creative phase gets underway is much easier to adapt than concept design. It also serves as a clear guide for a functionality plan, setting milestones, and producing timelines.

My biggest challenge is getting the approver to take that deep dive in the wireframe (wearing the user’s shoes and not their own), to go from start to finish and interact with every page and clickable element, and get the full-blown experience.

Step four

Get in front of real users early

We try to get inside the head of users, but we all process information differently. The importance of never making assumptions and getting real people in front of our product (at the wireframe stage) is worth the extra time and effort. What may seem obvious to us is not necessarily intuitive for everyone.

I used the form below to gather information from a group of testers engaging with our intranet interactive wireframe early on in the project.

Testing users for me ensures that the product served up offers the best kind of experience... one that engages and triggers some sort of emotion, be it excitement or curiosity or something entirely different. The experience can help guide the user quickly and effortlessly to get where they want to go (but really it's about where you want them to go). 

Step five

We have a plan… Let's start the fun stuff!

Solid research and planning results in smooth flow for the rest of the project. Massive amounts of work can be accomplished at this stage. The development team works on a functionality plan. The design team starts the UI mockup and once approved; development can begin.

This is my favorite stage (progress steps up a notch). And of course, for every hour of work at this stage, there are 2-3 hours of testing and QC (not as fun… but a necessary evil). But what fun it is to see everything come together at the end. A successful launch might even warrant a victory happy dance (some may jump up and do a full dance. I tend to attempt a sit-at-my-desk-waving-my-arms kind of dance instead)!
